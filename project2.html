Background

In Fall 2020, I was a student in the Innovation and Design Thinking course taught at the Haas School of Business at UC Berkeley. For our semester-long project, students were placed into groups and assigned a problem space to design a business solution for. Our team consisted of 6 team members. All team members were equally involved in the development of the solution. Another team member and I worked on the app design for Sustainable Hand using Figma.

Problem Statement

We’d like you to meet Abby.

Abby has always wanted to purchase more sustainably, but with her hectic schedule, it's hard to make time to explore the world of sustainable shopping. She’s also recently adopted a child and is now a parent who cares even more about the future her child will be raised in.

She’s found it difficult to fulfill household needs with popular online sustainable shops. Most of these shops aren’t really family focused. And regarding children’s products? Well, there may be a crayon here and a teething toy there, but not much else.

For people, like Abby, who aren't participating in the sustainability movement due to high costs, limited time, and lack of knowledge, there must be a better way to encourage sustainable household practices.

HMW Statement

The HMW Statement that we honed in on as a team is: How might we help young parents that have limited time, resources, knowledge and participate in the sustainability movement?

Solution

In comes Sustainable Hand. Sustainable Hand is an app that is a one-stop sustainable shop filled with affordable household solutions curated with young families in mind but open to all. Our app aims to limit the amount of hopping around that a customer would have to do to find sustainable and affordable household solutions. With the fun educational resources we provide on the platform, customers can educate themselves and engage their children to develop lifelong sustainable practices.

Research Approach

Our original problem statement was, “For people who aren't participating in the sustainability movement due to high costs, limited time, and lack of knowledge, there must be a better way to encourage sustainable household practices.“

To answer this, we began by independently conducting a STEEP analysis. After we discussed our independent insights, we developed diverse list of interviewees. For each interviewee, we developed questions to understand their perception of sustainability and how they try to be sustainable in their households. If they are not sustainable, we wanted to understand why that was the case. Each group member was assigned two interviewees, resulting in a total of 12 interviews.

To the left, you can see the affinity map that our team created based off of the insights we independently realized through our STEEP analyses and interviews. Some themes apparent in the map include how the cost of sustainable was concerning for customers, customers experienced guilt for not doing enough or doing things wrong, and more. We concluded that the overall theme of the board should be that “Sustainability is chaotic”.

Research Insights

Time Efficient

We noticed that a common trend among similar interviewees was that they lack time, lack the time to clean the house let alone research sustainable practices, and for a person like Abby, time is of the essence. Our solution must be time efficient for our sake and for our users.

Fear of Failure

To build on that lack of time, young parents like Abby have an overwhelming fear of failure due to the excess of information on sustainable practices. Because of this, we needed to ask ourselves, how can we make information both effective, and easy to comprehend?

Observable Impact

Lastly, it is important for parents to know they are making an impact and not wasting their time and money. We realized they want to feel like they are a good person when using our product. These insights were large contributors to the development of features within our solution.

Usability Testing Approach

Our plan for usability testing was to show our prototype to young parents from urban areas (our target audience) and get their feedback while having them navigate the app interface. The primary task assigned to our users was to explore the interface and search for a product to purchase. Each team member was assigned to conduct usability testing with two users, resulting in a total of 12 usability tests.

Testing Insights

Desire to Learn

We noticed that a common trend among similar interviewees was that they lack time, lack the time to clean the house let alone research sustainable practices, and for a person like Abby, time is of the essence. Our solution must be time efficient for our sake and for our users.

Confused about Composting

Through our interviews, we found that most were confused about the composting feature. While some wished there was more information about the service, others questioned its purpose and indicated they would likely not use it.

Explanation of Reward System

The final insight from our testing was that there was not enough information about how the reward system worked. This is important to our project because the reward system was meant to help us retain customers.

Introducing Sustainable Hand

With Sustainable Hand, we make sustainability a more accessible choice for households to adopt, thus helping us leave the environment a better place than we found it.

Young parents don’t have time to think about sustainability. We created a feature that compares similar products from different brands side-by-side. We do all the research to help our users make greener choices with minimal effort.

Because users wanted an educational component for the family, we developed a homepage full of articles, videos, tips, and kid-friendly content so that they can learn about how their purchases impact the Earth. For people like Abby, this is critical because she wants her child to learn about sustainability.

Customers are rewarded based on the impact they are creating, not necessarily the amount they are purchasing. A progress bar will also help users keep track of their total impact points and view the rewards they qualify for. This helps the users to feel like they're making a difference.

Outcomes

At the end of the Fall 2020 semester, our team presented our solution at the final showcase for the course. Our presentation received positive feedback from both the instructors and guests in attendance. The Sustainable Hand project concluded in December 2020.

Reflection

Working on the Sustainable Hand project was an informative experience. Throughout the journey of completing this project, I feel that I have become stronger at the ideation and user research processes. I am much more comfortable brainstorming and experimenting with potential solutions. I have also become much more assured in my interviewing skills and am no longer as nervous when conducting interviews.

A potential area of growth that I identified for myself would be to become more experienced with developing a consistent design system. I usually complete design tasks independently and have had limited opportunities to work other designers. For this project, I worked with another designer. I found the final interface to be stylistically inconsistent. Despite agreeing on elements such as font family, font size, color palette, and more, our screens look different from one another. In the future, I hope to learn how to maintain a consistent design style when working with other designers.

Another area of growth for myself is to learn how to create distinct designs. After reflecting on the screens I created, I found that they looked quite similar to the screens for the Impactify app. Even though we selected different palettes and font families for this project, the apps appear to resemble one another. I am eager to learn how I can have a unique style as a designer without creating designs that appear too similar to one another.
